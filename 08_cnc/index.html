Week 6: Casting Dry Electrodes

This week’s assignment was to use a CNC process, to create a design and post-process it through molding, casting, vacuum forming, or composites. After discussing with Nathan and the TFS, I slightly pivoted from direct CNC machining. Instead, I focused on building molds and casting to solve a real-world challenge for our livestock EMS system, building a reusable “dry” electrode that works without conductive gel.

Problem: Gel Electrodes Won’t Work on our Cows

Most EMS systems use gel-based disposable electrodes. But these are impractical in the field. The gel dries out quickly, creating painful “hotspot” sensations and inconsistent contact, especially problematic when trying to stimulate through a thick coat of animal hair.

To address this, I needed to develop a reusable electrode that:

  • Doesn’t dry out
  • Can pass current through fur
  • Feels safe and smooth on the skin
  • Is robust and field-usable

Designing the Mold

Instead of using CNC milling, I used a resin 3D printer to create fine-detail molds. My electrode design includes 1mm contact pins, which are simply too small and delicate for CNC tooling. SLA printing gave me the precision I needed to mold around those features.

I created a series of iterations:

  • The first mold held a flat electrode puck
  • Later versions introduced pin-like protrusions to help penetrate fur
  • I added cavities for holding different materials inside the mold

Exploring Dry Conductive Materials

The second part of the challenge was finding a material that could conduct electricity without needing water-based gels. Here’s what I tested:

  1. Glycerol – Hygroscopic and skin-safe, but not naturally conductive enough
  2. Glycerol + Choline Chloride – A eutectic solvent (Google taught me that word) that increases ion availability, making the contact better with skin
  3. Polyurethane + Graphene Powder – The idea was to make a flexible, moldable conductive elastomer. However, graphene disrupted the polyurethane cure and remained too resistive
  4. Graphene Conductive Paint – This worked surprisingly well for surface coating, but wasn’t viable through fur since it couldn’t hold enough hygroscopic material
  5. Agarose + Glycerol & Choline Chloride – Agarose helped hold the solution together, and I could pour it into the mold and let it set. The result was a flexible, semi-gelled puck that could conform to skin and hold enough glycerol

Final Test: It Worked (Mostly)

I brought the electrode to a test on a farm and applied it to the cow. I wasn’t able to get the graphene-coated electrode to trigger a strong response through the coat—it wasn’t conductive enough. But the agarose/glycerol blend worked. It made enough contact to trigger visible muscle contraction without discomfort.

Learnings

  • Resin printing is a powerful alternative to CNC for small, high-precision molds
  • Conductivity in flexible materials is harder than expected, there’s a tradeoff between mechanical properties and electrical performance
  • Agarose is a surprisingly good material for flexible electrodes, it holds shape and retains moisture, without being sticky
  • Iteration is key: I went through at least 4 failed attempts before finding a combo that worked

Next Steps

  • Refine the electrode mold design for better surface conformity
  • Maybe explore conductive silicones like EcoFlex + carbon black or silver powder
  • Design a snap-in connector to easily swap electrodes in and out of the collar
